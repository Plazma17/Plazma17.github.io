Sales Call Playbook
SAT Tutoring - How to handle every call from first contact to close
Iron Rules
These apply to every single call, no exceptions.
Always Do
Ask questions before you say anything about yourself or pricing. Understand them first.
Never Do
Give a price before you know their situation. Never. Even if they ask directly, redirect first.
Always Do
Close toward the next small step, not the full sale. Get them to a consultation, not a payment.
Never Do
Use filler words: um, uh, like, you know, sort of. Pause silently instead. Silence sounds confident.
Always Do
Sound calm and certain. You know the SAT better than this parent. Own that.
Never Do
Volunteer information they didn't ask for. Answer what was asked, then stop talking.
The Call Flow
Follow this sequence every time. Do not skip steps.
1
Opening
Answer and take control immediately
Pick up confidently. State your name. Then immediately flip it to them with a question so you control the conversation from the start.
Say This "Hey, this is Romaan. Thanks for reaching out. Before I tell you about what I offer, can I ask a few quick questions about your son or daughter?"
2
Qualify
Understand exactly who you're dealing with
Ask these four questions in order. Listen carefully. Take mental notes. This tells you what kind of student and what kind of close you need.
Ask In Order 1. "What grade is your son/daughter in?"

2. "Have they taken the SAT yet? Do you have a score?"

3. "Do you have a target score in mind, or a specific college they're aiming for?"

4. "Have they done any SAT prep before, or would this be starting from scratch?"
3
Educate If Needed
If they don't understand why SAT matters, tell them
Some parents, especially newer immigrants or those unfamiliar with US college admissions, genuinely don't know how much SAT scores matter. This is your opportunity. Inform them, don't lecture them.
If They Seem Unaware "Just so you have the full picture - for most competitive colleges, SAT scores are still one of the biggest factors in admissions. A 100 to 200 point improvement can genuinely change which schools your son gets accepted to. That's what we focus on - targeted improvement in the areas that are actually hurting the score."
4
Position Yourself
Now briefly explain who you are and why it matters
Keep this short. One or two sentences. Your score is the credential. Let it speak.
Say This "I'm a Zionsville senior. I scored 1550 on the SAT - 790 Math, 760 Reading and Writing. I've been through this exact test recently, so I know exactly where students lose points and how to fix it."
5
Handle Pricing
If they ask about price, redirect - don't answer yet
You cannot give a fair price without seeing the student's score report. This is genuinely true, so say it. It's not evasive, it's accurate.
If They Ask Price "Honestly it depends on where your son is starting and what we're trying to reach. I don't want to give you a number that doesn't make sense for your situation. Once I see a score report I can tell you exactly what kind of plan we're looking at and what it costs. Can he take a practice test this week?"
6
Close
Always close toward the free consultation, not the sale
The goal of this call is one thing: get them to agree to a 20-minute free consultation. That's it. Don't try to sell the full package on a cold call.
Say This "How about this - let's set up a free 20-minute call with you and your son so I can hear where he's at and what he's working toward. No commitment, no pressure. If it seems like a good fit after that we can talk about next steps. Does that work for you?"
When They Say This
Common objections and exactly how to respond.
They Say You Say
"Just give me a rough number" "I genuinely can't give you a fair number without knowing where your son is starting. It would either be too high or too low for your situation. Let me look at the score report first."
"We already have a tutor" "That's totally fine. If things ever change or you want a second opinion on the approach, feel free to reach back out."
"Is this a real tutoring company?" "I'm a local Zionsville senior offering private one-on-one tutoring. I scored 1550 on the SAT and I work only with real College Board materials. It's personalized in a way most tutoring centers can't match."
"My son doesn't know what score he got" "No problem. He can take a free official College Board practice test at home and we can use that as our starting point. I'll score it and we go from there."
"We'll think about it" "Of course. Just so you know, spots are limited before the May test date. If you want to lock something in, the free consultation doesn't cost anything and there's no commitment. Want to put something on the calendar now just in case?"
"Can you guarantee a score increase?" "I can't guarantee a specific number because it depends on how much work the student puts in between sessions. What I can tell you is that every student I work with gets a personalized plan targeting their exact weak areas using only real SAT questions. That's the most effective way to improve."

Student Types and How to Handle Them
Every inquiry falls into one of these buckets. Identify fast, adjust approach.
A
Type
The motivated student with a clear target
Knows their score, knows their target school, just needs help. Easiest close. Go straight to building a plan and discussing sessions.
B
Type
The parent who has no idea
Like your first call. Doesn't know score, doesn't know target, may not fully understand SAT importance. Educate first. Ask if their child is a citizen or international student - international students need stronger scores and this creates urgency. Then redirect to a consultation.
C
Type
The price shopper
First question is about cost. Don't give it. Redirect to understanding their situation. If they keep pushing after you've explained your approach, give the hourly rate confidently and don't apologize for it.
D
Type
The comparison shopper
Already looking at other tutors or centers. Differentiate clearly: "Most tutoring centers use third-party practice materials. I use only real College Board questions - the exact same ones that appear on the actual test. That matters a lot for score improvement."
