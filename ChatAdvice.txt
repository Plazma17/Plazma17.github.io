# SAT Tutoring Sales Playbook

## 1) Goal of the call

The goal is **not** to dump price immediately. The goal is to:

1. Learn what the student needs.
2. Make the parent feel understood.
3. Explain the process clearly.
4. Give the price only after the value is clear.

## 2) What to sound like

* Calm
* Clear
* Confident
* Not rushed
* Not defensive
* Not salesy

## 3) Main call structure

### Step 1: Opening

Say:

* “Hi, this is [Name]. Thanks for reaching out.”
* “Before I explain anything, can I ask a couple quick questions about your student’s goals?”

Why:

* Starts polite
* Makes the parent talk first
* Gives you control without sounding pushy

### Step 2: Find out the student’s situation

Ask:

* “Has your student taken the SAT yet?”
* “What score are they aiming for?”
* “What section is harder for them, Math or Reading/Writing?”
* “When are they planning to take the test?”

Why:

* You need the problem before you can pitch the solution
* Different students need different plans

### Step 3: Reflect back what you heard

Say something like:

* “Got it. So the main goal is to raise the score in a focused way before the test date.”
* “That makes sense. In that case, the first thing I’d do is identify the weakest areas and build from there.”

Why:

* Shows you were listening
* Makes you sound competent
* Makes the parent feel understood

### Step 4: Explain your process

Say:

* “I start with a diagnostic review so I can see exactly where points are being lost.”
* “Then I build a simple plan around the student’s weak areas and use real SAT-style practice to target those skills.”
* “I keep sessions focused and make sure the student knows what to work on next.”

Why:

* Parents buy structure
* A process feels more professional than “I just tutor”

### Step 5: Mention your credibility briefly

Say:

* “My partner and I have both scored in the 1500s on the SAT, so we know the test well.”
* “We focus on practical strategies, not just random practice.”

Why:

* This is enough proof
* Do not turn it into bragging
* Keep it short and factual

### Step 6: Give price only after value is clear

Say:

* “Based on what you’re describing, I’d probably recommend starting with a short plan and seeing how the student responds.”
* “If that sounds like a fit, I can explain the pricing and next steps.”

Why:

* You do not want to lead with price
* You want the parent to already be interested before you say a number

## 4) What to say when they ask about price too early

If they say: “How much is it?”
Say:

* “I can give you the exact pricing, but first I want to make sure I understand what your student needs so I do not give you the wrong recommendation.”
* “The plan depends on the score goal and how much prep time we have.”

Why:

* This buys you time
* It keeps the call focused on their student, not on haggling

## 5) What to say if they compare you to bigger companies

If they say: “How are you different from a tutoring company?”
Say:

* “We keep it more personal and focused.”
* “You are not getting a generic one-size-fits-all system.”
* “We build the plan around the student directly.”

Why:

* This is your advantage
* You are small, so use that as a strength

## 6) What to say if they sound unsure

If they say:

* “We’re just looking around.”
* “We are not sure yet.”
* “We need to think about it.”

Say:

* “That makes sense.”
* “If helpful, I can tell you what I would do first for a student in that situation.”
* “Even one short session can show what the student needs most.”

Why:

* Keeps the conversation alive
* Gives them a low-pressure next step

## 7) What to avoid

Do not:

* Ramble
* Apologize too much
* Give a price before understanding the student
* Sound desperate
* Overexplain every detail
* Use filler words like “uh,” “like,” “you know,” and “basically” too much
* Sound fake or overly corporate

## 8) Follow-up message after the call

If they do not buy right away, send:

“Thanks for talking earlier. Based on what you shared, I think the student would benefit most from [weak area]. If you want, I can help map out a simple plan from there.”

Why:

* Friendly
* Useful
* Not pushy
* Keeps the door open

## 9) Simple rule for both of you

Before saying a price, make sure you have said:

1. What problem the student has
2. How you solve it
3. Why your approach is solid

If those three are not clear yet, keep talking and asking questions.
